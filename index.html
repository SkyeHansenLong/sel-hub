Why SEL Matters Across the Lifespan

Social and emotional learning begins before birth and is shaped by early caregiving, access to health care, and consistent developmental opportunities. High-quality prenatal care and responsive early environments influence the stress-response system, secure attachment, and executive functions that later affect adolescents’ identity, peer relationships, and decision making (Ormrod, Anderman, & Anderman, 2024).

  • Prenatal care & early experiences → adolescent outcomes: Stable caregiving and access to prenatal/early supports reduce toxic stress, building resilience that helps teens handle peer pressure and identity work.
  • Caregiver well-being matters: Parent stress changes across a child’s developmental stages, and that climate shows up in the classroom (Luthar & Ciciolla, 2016).
  • Systems perspective: Cross-disciplinary decisions during pregnancy and infancy (health, social work, education) can buffer risk and set the stage for positive SEL (Mc Elhinney, Taylor, & Sinclair, 2021; Lu et al., 2001; Tough et al., 2007). Arizona’s SEL Competencies and the Arizona Early Learning Standards (AZELS) give us a framework to build these skills on purpose, not by accident.

Quick Start: Use Tomorrow

  1. Create a daily 2–3 minute regulation ritual (breathing, stretch, or brief journaling).
  2. Teach 5–7 emotion words explicitly; post visuals and sentence stems children can actually use.
  3. Embed problem-solving role-plays in centers, small groups, and core lessons.

Small, repeated practice wires skills more than one-time lessons.

Effect of Early Development & Care on Adolescent SEL

High-quality prenatal care and responsive early caregiving reduce toxic stress and support secure attachment and executive functions. These early foundations predict healthier adolescent identity work, peer relationships, and self-management in school settings (Ormrod et al., 2024; Luthar & Ciciolla, 2016). Arizona’s SEL Competencies and AZELS (Arizona Department of Education) give schools and families a shared language for skills like self-awareness, self-management, and relationship skills all the way from birth through K–12.

  • Stress buffering: Reliable co-regulation in infants and toddlers helps “tune” the child’s stress system so it is less explosive in adolescence.
  • Attachment → belonging: Early secure attachment predicts stronger feelings of classroom belonging and healthier conflict resolution with peers later.
  • Executive functions: Early practice with impulse control and working memory lays the groundwork for middle-childhood self-control and teen goal setting.

SEL Milestones & Strategies by Stage

Stages highlighted here: Infancy (0–12 months), Toddlerhood (1–3 years), Early Childhood (3–5 years). Each stage lines up with Arizona Early Learning Standards (social-emotional strands “Self” and “Relationships”) and the Arizona SEL Competencies.

Infancy (0–12 months)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones

  • Attachment bonds; social smile (~6–8 weeks); stranger anxiety (~8–10 months).
  • Beginnings of self-soothing with adult co-regulation (rocking, calm voice, pacifier); brief independent calming appears late in infancy.
  • Joint attention (looking/pointing with caregiver), which lays a foundation for language and shared understanding.

Classroom / Program SEL Strategies

  • Responsive routines: warm greetings, mirroring baby sounds, and prompt comfort to build secure attachment.
  • Serve-and-return: short face-to-face games (peekaboo, copying facial expressions) to build trust and early reciprocity.

Family Strategies (Home)

  • Co-regulation: predictable feeding/sleep routines; narrate feelings (“You’re tired, we’re going to rock and breathe together”).
  • Sensory-smart soothing: swaddling, gentle motion, white noise as appropriate; slowly lengthen pauses to build tiny moments of self-settling.

Toddlerhood (1–3 years)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones

  • Big feelings + first impulse control with adult scaffolding (“stop,” “wait”).
  • Emerging self-awareness (“me/mine”); parallel → associative play; early sharing and turn-taking with coaching.
  • Emotion vocabulary grows (“happy,” “mad,” “scared”); toddlers start basic problem-solving with adult prompts.

Classroom SEL Strategies (Teachers)

  • Emotion coaching + visuals: teach a simple 3-step “Name it → Breathe → Do,” using picture cards and a cozy calm corner.
  • Guided transitions: songs, visual timers, and “first-then” boards; practice waiting and turn-taking in centers.

Family Strategies (Home)

  • Two-choice limits: offer two acceptable options (“blue cup or red cup?”) to reduce power struggles and build autonomy.
  • Calm-down basket: soft toy, chunky book, squeeze ball. Practice using it during calm times so it’s familiar when emotions spike.

Early Childhood (3–5 years)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones

  • Broader emotion words; begins perspective-taking in cooperative play.
  • Improves impulse control; follows multi-step routines with cues; can recover from disappointment with strategies.
  • Builds friendships; negotiates rules; understands fairness and simple empathy.

Classroom SEL Strategies (Teachers)

  • Morning feelings check-in: kids point to an emoji/face or color zone, then everyone does a short regulation warm-up (breathing, stretch).
  • Role-play & social stories: act out “asking to join,” “turn-taking,” and “fix-it plans.” This directly matches AZELS social-emotional standards.

Family Strategies (Home)

  • Name-it-to-tame-it: label feelings during routines and story time; pair each feeling with a strategy (count to 5, wall push, get a drink).
  • “Chorelets” (tiny helper jobs): napkin helper, book buddy, plant waterer. These build pride, empathy, and responsibility.

Infant–Toddler Care Routines = SEL (Use in Early Care Rooms)

Routines are the curriculum. Use predictable language, warm tone, and gentle physical presence to turn diapers, mealtimes, arrivals, and naps into moments of co-regulation and trust.

Arrival / Good-bye

  • “I’m happy you’re here. Let’s hang your coat, then choose a cozy book.”
  • Visual routine card + comfort object + 30-second calm snuggle.

Diapering / Toileting

  • “Sports-caster” talk (“I’m lifting you now… warm wipe… all done”) builds safety and language.
  • Offer simple choices (“Wipes or tissue first?”) to build agency.

Feeding / Mealtimes

  • Serve-and-return: pause, watch their face/gesture, respond to the cue.
  • “Pause spoon, breathe… your turn.” Practice waiting & turn-taking.

Sleep / Nap Transitions

  • 2-minute wind-down: low light, soft hum, two shared deep breaths.
  • Gentle wake-up script: “Good afternoon, stretch your body, sip of water, let’s get a book.”

Aligned with AZELS Social-Emotional (Self; Relationships) and Arizona SEL Competencies.

What Is Self-Regulation?

Self-regulation is the ability to monitor and adjust our thoughts, emotions, and behaviors so we can meet goals and follow shared expectations. It depends on executive functions (attention, impulse control, working memory) and emotion regulation. A regulated child can: pause before reacting in conflict, use a coping strategy when anxious, and stick with a hard task by breaking it into steps (Ormrod et al., 2024).

How Teachers Model & Encourage It

  • Think-alouds: “I feel frustrated, so I’m going to take three breaths and split this job into two parts.” Kids need to hear adults narrate calm problem-solving.
  • Routine practice: A tiny daily regulation warm-up (breathing, stretch, mantra) at the same time every day builds predictability.
  • Goal-setting & reflection: Two-minute end-of-day check-ins: “Which strategy helped you calm today?” This links strategy → success.

Home Practices to Support Regulation

Share these ideas with families in simple, encouraging language. They match what you’re doing in class.

  • Co-regulate first: Adult calm comes before child calm. Soften the voice, slow your body, get close, then coach.
  • Name + strategy: “You’re frustrated. Let’s squeeze the ball and count to 5.” Always pair the feeling with a tool.
  • Predictable routines: Morning/bedtime picture charts and visual timers lower power struggles and build independence.
  • Repair & reconnect: After conflict: “I yelled. I’m sorry. Let’s try again.” That models accountability and restores safety.
